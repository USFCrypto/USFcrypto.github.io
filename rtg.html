Overview

The NSF Research Training Group (RTG) in Applied Algebra at USF is designed to build a durable research and training ecosystem around foundational mathematics with strategic applications. The program focuses on three tightly connected areas: cryptography, coding theory, and quantum computing.

Its central idea is simple: research training is most powerful when it is vertically integrated. Instead of separating students by level, the RTG creates teams in which undergraduates, graduate students, postdoctoral associates, and faculty collaborate on common mathematical questions, learn from one another, and grow within a supportive research culture.

The RTG builds on major strengths already developed at USF, including the USF Center for Cryptographic Research, the USF Quantum Initiative, the REU program in Cryptography and Coding Theory, and a growing applied algebra curriculum that connects mathematics to urgent national needs in cybersecurity, reliable data storage, and quantum information science.

The RTG Model

Vertically Integrated Teams

Each research team is designed to include a postdoctoral associate, a graduate student, undergraduate researchers, and faculty mentors. This structure creates a genuine research pipeline rather than isolated activities.

Three Research Pillars

The program is organized around cryptography, coding theory, and quantum computing, with strong mathematical connections between them and with opportunities for cross-pollination across projects.

Professional Development

In addition to research, the RTG emphasizes communication, mentoring, teaching, writing, collaboration, and leadership so that trainees leave as stronger, more versatile mathematicians.

Research Pillars

Cryptography

The cryptography team studies hard computational problems from algebra and number theory that can be used to design the next generation of secure systems, especially in the post-quantum setting.

  • Lattice-based cryptography
  • Code-based cryptography
  • Isogenies and group actions
  • Algebraic cryptanalysis
  • Nonlinear functions for symmetric cryptography

Coding Theory

The coding theory team develops mathematical tools for reliable communication and distributed storage, with particular emphasis on locally recoverable codes, decoding problems, and connections between codes and cryptography.

  • Error-correcting codes
  • Locally recoverable codes
  • Repair problems in storage systems
  • Decoding algorithms
  • Code equivalence and code-based security

Quantum Computing

The quantum computing team investigates quantum algorithms, algebraic methods for circuit synthesis, and the interplay between quantum computation, number theory, group theory, and coding theory.

  • Quantum algorithms in number theory
  • Exact and approximate gate synthesis
  • Computational group theory for circuits
  • Quantum error correction
  • Mathematical foundations of quantum information

Florida Hub for Applied Algebra

A distinctive feature of the RTG is its commitment to building a broader statewide community around Applied Algebra. Through the Florida Hub for Applied Algebra, the program fosters collaborations with faculty and students across Florida institutions, including minority-serving institutions and partner universities.

This hub supports workshops, shared mentoring, collaborative research discussions, and new bridges between mathematics and neighboring disciplines.

Why This Program Matters

Applied Algebra now sits at the heart of several fast-moving strategic areas: post-quantum cybersecurity, resilient digital infrastructure, and quantum information science. The RTG responds to this moment by training mathematicians who are not only technically strong, but also collaborative, adaptable, and prepared to contribute across academia, industry, and government.

The program is also designed to broaden participation in these fields by creating a supportive environment and strengthening recruitment and retention pathways for students from groups historically underrepresented in the mathematical sciences.
